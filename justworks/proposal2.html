Section 01

Problem Overview, Context & Business Opportunity

The Agent’s Reality Today
CSO agents spend the first few minutes of every call piecing together customer context across 5–6 disconnected systems — instead of actually helping the customer.

Justworks has earned NPS +60 and 9 Stevie Awards at ~10,000 customers. That quality was built on human heroics — agents who are exceptional despite the tooling, not because of it. But heroics don’t scale. As the customer base grows, either headcount climbs proportionally or quality erodes.

The data to serve customers brilliantly already exists — it’s just scattered across systems that were never designed to talk to each other. Nobody knows the overall health of an individual customer. Nobody knows the overall health of the book. The signals are there; the synthesis is not.

5–6 systems per call
No single source of truth
Disparate, siloed data
No customer health visibility
No smart pattern recognition
Can’t surface product opportunities
Reactive at best

We traced 6 distinct problems back to the exact language in the case study brief. Problems 1–4 map directly to the Phase 1 MVP; problems 5–6 require the data foundation that the MVP builds, and are addressed in Phases 2–3.

P1

The Context-Switching Tax

Agents toggle between 5–6 disconnected systems (Zendesk, Salesforce, admin dashboard, knowledge base, Slack, Tableau) to assemble a complete picture. Every switch costs cognitive bandwidth and clock time.

Case Study Brief
“Customer Central should allow CSO to carry out routine tasks, understand complete profiles about our customer companies and the employees of those companies, and ultimately give them information necessary to provide a best-in-class customer experience for small businesses.”
P2

The Caller Identity Problem

An agent may only have a phone number. They must determine: admin or employee? Which company? What history? Two caller types require entirely different data. The first 30–60 seconds are wasted on identification.

Case Study Brief
“What data and tooling in a Customer Health Dashboard would be relevant for a customer support team member to fully understand the profile of the person, and their company, during an encounter?”
P3

The Repeat-Yourself Problem

Customer contacted Monday by email, Wednesday by chat, calls Friday — told their story three times. Interaction history is split between Zendesk and Salesforce in formats not consumable during a live call.

Case Study Brief
“Customer Central should allow CSO to carry out routine tasks, understand complete profiles about our customer companies and the employees of those companies, and ultimately give them information necessary to provide a best-in-class customer experience for small businesses.”
P4

The Account Status Blindspot

Agents need billing status, next payroll date, recent account changes — but this data lives in production DB and billing systems, not in Salesforce or Zendesk. Requires a separate system login.

Case Study Brief
“We aim to ensure our CSO is informed enough to handle the direct problems they will encounter on a call.”
P5

The Proactive Blindspot

Justworks wants CSO to pre-empt problems and surface product opportunities. Usage patterns, lifecycle events, and adoption gaps exist across systems but are never synthesized into agent-facing signals.

Case Study Brief
“Our customer interactions — calls, emails, chat — serve as an informative proprietary data set for us to learn from and action off of within our tooling.”
Case Study Brief
“We aim to ensure our CSO is informed enough to handle the direct problems they will encounter on a call, but also pre-empt problems before they arise and proactively provide support and services that the customer may not know they need yet, including opportunities to utilize additional areas of our product offerings.”
P6

The Open Issues Visibility Gap

No way to see active open tickets, pending tasks, or unresolved items for an account. An agent may spend 5 minutes diagnosing an issue another team is already working on, or make conflicting commitments.

Derived from Brief
Derived from the brief’s requirement that CSO “carry out routine tasks” and “understand complete profiles.” An agent cannot manage open work if they cannot see it. Also informed by the JTBD analysis: “Hand Off Cleanly — when I can’t fully resolve an issue, I want to capture enough context that the next person can pick up seamlessly.”
The Solution
Customer Central puts the complete customer picture — identity, history, account status, and a health score that instantly signals what’s working and what’s not — on one screen in under 3 seconds, so the agent walks into every conversation already knowing where to focus.

This wasn’t designed by starting with features. I decomposed the case study brief into 6 jobs CSO agents need to perform, inventoried the top problems across Justworks’ product surface, and discovered that the highest-impact problems and health-status questions independently converged on the same six data categories — that convergence defined the MVP scope. Every design decision is framed as a hypothesis waiting to be validated; the full 14-step decision chain traces each choice back to the brief.

From disparate systems and reactive firefighting to a single, unified workspace that assembles context before the customer finishes saying hello. No more toggling. No more asking “can you give me your company name?” No more flying blind on account health.

And that’s just the immediate win. The real unlock is what comes next: the same data foundation that powers instant context becomes the platform for preemptive support, predictive intelligence, and cross-functional insight — connecting the dots across customer interactions to inform sales, product, and the broader business.

Those are the problems — and that’s the solution. Here is why solving them represents a compounding strategic advantage, not just an operational fix.

The North Star
The customer never needed to call in the first place.

Not because Justworks eliminated human support — but because the system was so intelligent, so anticipatory, that the problem was resolved before the customer recognized it was a problem.

And when they do reach out, the interaction is so fast, so precise, so contextually aware that it feels like talking to someone who’s been managing their account personally for years — whether that’s a human, an AI, or a seamless blend of both.

The Scaling Equation

Logarithmic Support, Linear Customers

10,000 to 50,000 customers does not mean 5× the agents. It means the same team — or marginally larger — operating with exponentially better leverage because the tooling handles pattern recognition, context assembly, proactive outreach, and routine resolution.

The ratio of customer interactions to customer count declines quarter over quarter — not because service gets worse, but because the system gets smarter. Fewer reactive contacts. Faster resolution. A growing percentage of routine interactions handled by AI at a quality bar indistinguishable from the best human agents.

Section 02

Phased Roadmap

Customer Central is delivered in 3 phases, where each phase earns the right to the next. Phase 1 proves the data is accurate and agents trust it. Phase 2 upgrades trust into action. Phase 3 turns action into prediction. Skipping a phase isn't just risky — it's architecturally impossible because each layer depends on outputs from the one below.

Phase 1 spans 7 calendar weeks: 2 weeks of foundation-building (data spikes + core features), 2 weeks of validation (alpha with 5 agents, beta with a full pod), and a final week of full rollout with training. Every week ends with a decision gate. If the data isn't accurate enough or agents don't trust it, we extend rather than push forward.

Section 03

Phase 1 MVP Requirements & Acceptance Criteria

The MVP delivers 11 features across 3 categories: Foundation (the base layer every interaction needs), Problem (targeted at high-volume call types), and Health (composite scoring and leadership visibility). Every feature has documented acceptance criteria that are testable in the interactive prototype.

Of the 11 features, 8 are full builds and 3 are deep-link integrations that surface existing system data with contextual annotations. The deep-link approach covers ~80% of each use case at ~20% of the build cost — and the full build ships in Phase 2 once the data patterns are validated.

Section 04

Data Requirements

Customer Central aggregates 55 data fields from 5 source systems into a unified Customer Context API. Rather than querying sources in real-time for every interaction, we use a 4-tier loading strategy: instant (pre-cached identity + status in <500ms), fast (interaction timeline in <2s), background (tax/benefits badges loaded silently), and on-click (pay stubs and invoices fetched live on demand).

The top integration risks center on cross-system joins. The #1 question — whether a shared key exists between Zendesk Organization IDs and Salesforce Account IDs — determines whether interaction history merging is a 2-day or 2-week task. A Week 1 spike with Caroline Crossland's Salesforce team resolves this before it can block the timeline.

Top 3 Integration Risks

1. Zendesk ↔ Salesforce Join Key — shared identifier between systems may not exist; gates interaction history merge.
2. Payroll Engine Access — real-time payroll status may not be queryable via API; affects PS-1 and PS-2.
3. Payment-to-Payroll Business Logic — rules for how payment failure impacts payroll are undocumented; affects PS-6 cascade.

Section 05

Engineering Resources

The team is a 5+1 structure: 2 full-stack engineers split by component vertical (not layer), 1 Data Services data engineer for the highest-risk Salesforce pipeline, 1 product designer, 1 PM, and 1 shared QA engineer. Total investment is ~23 engineer-weeks over 5 build weeks.

Work is split by vertical, not layer. Engineer 1 owns search, identity, and the status strip — from database queries through API to React UI. Engineer 2 owns interaction timeline, all problem panels, and health scoring. Zero handoffs between "backend" and "frontend" engineers. The Data Services team handles the highest-risk dependency: getting Salesforce data flowing reliably into the Customer Context API.

What I Don’t Know

This proposal is thorough, but it is built from the outside in. I want to be upfront about the gaps:

  • I haven’t sat with a CSO agent — every workflow assumption comes from the case study brief, the help center, and industry patterns, not firsthand observation
  • I don’t know the actual top call drivers by volume — the problem inventory is inferred, not measured
  • I don’t know the real data architecture — the integration plan assumes standard APIs; the actual schema could change the Phase 1 timeline
  • Every UX decision in the prototype is an informed assumption — not validated by a single user test

That’s exactly why the proposal treats every design choice as a hypothesis waiting to be validated, and why Phase 1 starts with a paired spike to confirm the data architecture before a single line of product code is written. The full set of 45 assumptions is documented and traceable.

Section 07

Success Metrics & Rationale

Metrics are structured in 3 tiers. Outcome metrics (AHT reduction of 15–20%, FCR improvement of 10%, NPS per headcount dollar trending up) prove the business case at 90 days. Guardrail metrics (NPS ≥ +60, data accuracy ≥ 98%, no escalation rate increase) ensure we don't cause harm in pursuit of speed. Leading indicators (orientation time <10s, repeat-information rate <10%, systems accessed dropping from ~5 to ≤2) become visible in Week 1–2 and let us course-correct early.

The North Star metric — NPS per CSO headcount dollar — is deliberately chosen over raw NPS because it captures the leverage story: same quality, less cost per customer. This is the metric that proves tooling creates sustainable scale, not just incremental improvement.

Section 08

Rollout & Training Plan

Rollout follows a 3-stage pull-based model: Alpha (5 hand-picked agents, Week 5) → Beta (1 full pod of 20–30 agents, Week 6) → Full Rollout (all CSO agents, Week 7). Each stage has explicit gate criteria that must pass before advancing. If a gate fails, we extend by one week rather than push forward with unresolved issues.

Training is 3 layers: a 45-minute live demo (recorded, co-facilitated by a Champion), a self-paced sandbox with sample data, and a 1-page quick reference card printed at each workstation. The Champions program identifies 2–3 beta power users who become peer trainers during full rollout — agents learn best from agents, not from PMs.

Section 09

MVP Rationale

The MVP isn't the most important features first — it's the features that make every future feature possible. A 14-step decision chain moves from the raw prompt language through problem scoring, dependency analysis, and scoping cuts to arrive at exactly these 11 features. Each step builds on the previous one, and each is documented with its assumptions.

The core argument is architectural: you cannot build Phase 2 (in-place actions, ML scoring) without Phase 1's validated data layer. You cannot predict churn (Phase 3) without a health model agents trust (Phase 2). And you cannot build a health model without the unified data that Phase 1 consolidates. The phasing isn't a preference — it's a dependency chain.

"Read Before Write"

A dashboard that shows wrong data is a mild problem. A dashboard that lets agents write wrong data back into source systems is a severe problem. MVP is read-only. Write capabilities come after data accuracy is proven over 60+ days of production use.

Section 10

Out of Scope (Worth Revisiting)

Thirteen items are deliberately deferred, not forgotten. Each has a documented revisit trigger — a specific condition under which the deferral should be reconsidered. The key deferrals are in-place ticket creation (read path must prove trust first), benefits life event workflows (carrier/state complexity too high), compliance guidance (no structured content system exists), AI summaries (need 90+ days of structured records), and cross-functional intelligence — surfacing support interaction data to inform sales, product, and leadership decisions.

The principle: an item is out of scope when the cost of including it — complexity, timeline risk, cognitive load — exceeds the cost of deferring it. Every deferred item has a clear path back into the product once its prerequisites are met.

Anticipated Future Features
