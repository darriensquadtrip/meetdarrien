Customer Central · Interview Game Plan

Presentation Outline & Workshop Plan

75 minutes. Two modes. First you present, then you facilitate. The presentation earns permission to lead the workshop. The workshop is where the job is won.

Key calibration: They've already read your proposal. Don't re-read it to them. The presentation is about the reasoning behind the decisions — the thinking they can't get from the document alone. Move fast on what they've seen. Go deep on why.

01
The Agent's Reality — Lead With the Pain
Set the emotional foundation. Make the room feel the problem before you propose anything.
3 min ▼
What to Say

"Before I walk through the solution, I want to make sure we're grounded in the problem. I'm going to describe what I believe a CSO agent's day looks like today — and I want you to tell me in the workshop where I'm right and where I'm wrong."

What to Show

The encounter narrative. Walk through the "Today vs. Ideal" encounter — T+0s to T+120s of a call from Sarah at Acme Corp. Paint the picture: 6 tabs, 2 minutes of dead air, customer repeating themselves. Then the ideal: instant context, proactive acknowledgment, 30-second resolution.

Why This Opening

The rubric scores user empathy for CSO agents (Annelise), operational impact thinking (Andres), and strategic vision connected to business outcomes (Renee). Starting with the agent's pain — not a product feature list — signals that you understand the human problem. It also frames every subsequent decision as "this solves THAT."

02
Problem, Context & Business Opportunity
The 6 compounding problems and why solving them is a strategic investment, not a feature request.
3 min ▼
What to Say

Name the 6 compounding problems quickly — context-switching tax, caller identity, repeat yourself, knowledge gap, proactive blindspot, scaling threat. Don't elaborate on each. Say: "These are hypothesized based on domain patterns. Several of you work with CSO daily — I'll ask you to validate these in the workshop." Then connect to business: AHT reduction, FCR improvement, churn prevention, NPS defense, upsell enablement.

Panel Hooks

Renee: "Customer service is Justworks' moat. The moat either widens with tooling or erodes without it." Annelise: "These agents are consultants, not call center reps. The tools should match their caliber." Caroline: "We have the data — it's just trapped in 5 systems." Evan: "Every problem traces to a specific data gap we can close."

What to Avoid

Don't spend 5 minutes on company background. They know Justworks. Don't recite revenue numbers or product line details. State the strategic framing in 30 seconds and move to the problems — that's where your thinking shows.

03
How I Got to the MVP — The Decision Logic
The part they can't read in the doc. Show the reasoning, not just the output.
4 min ▼
What to Say

Walk through the logic chain at speed: "I started by extracting 6 jobs from the prompt language. I identified 20 specific problems CSO agents likely face and 12 health questions they need answered. I scored these on Impact, Confidence, and Ease — and discovered something interesting: both the problems and the health questions independently converge on the same 6 data categories. That convergence told me the foundation is sound. Then I applied a trust progression framework — agents must trust the data before they trust the analysis before they trust predictions. That gives us three phases, and each phase earns the right to the next."

Key Artifacts to Reference

The ICE scoring matrix — show the convergence visually. The trust progression — Phase 1 (accurate data) → Phase 2 (meaningful signals) → Phase 3 (actionable predictions). The Tiebreaker Principle: "When two features are equal, pick the one that also builds toward the AI-native future."

What This Proves

This is the moment Renee sees strategic thinking under ambiguity. You didn't start with features — you started with problems, scored them, found convergence, and derived the scope. That's the GPM thinking she's hiring for.

04
The MVP Scope — 9 Features, 3 Foundation + 6 Problem Stories
What's in, what's out, and why. This is where MVP discipline shows.
3 min ▼
What to Say

Name the 9 features fast: FS-1 Instant Caller Recognition, FS-2 Complete Interaction History, FS-3 Account Status Strip, PS-1 Pay Stub Context (full build), PS-2 Payroll Run Status (deep link), PS-3 Tax Document Access (deep link), PS-4 Benefits Quick-View (deep link), PS-5 Invoice Timeline (deep link), PS-6 Payment Cascade (deep link). Then explain the Full Build vs. Deep Link framework: "The 3 foundation features and PS-1 are full builds. The rest are deep links — one click to the source system. Deep links deliver 80% of the value at 20% of the cost."

What to Avoid

Don't read acceptance criteria aloud — that's in the document. Don't defend every scoping decision proactively. Let the panel challenge your scope. When they do, you're ready with the rationale. Being challenged and responding well scores higher than not being challenged at all.

Transition to Demo

"Let me show you what this feels like in practice." → Open the prototype. Walk through the Sarah Chen encounter: search → profile → status strip → interaction history → pay stub tab. 60 seconds maximum. It's a taste, not a tour — the workshop is where they'll explore it.

05
Data Architecture & Integration Realism
This is for Caroline and Evan. Show you know where the data lives.
3 min ▼
What to Say

"Five source systems: Production DB (companies, members, payroll, billing — source of truth), Salesforce (accounts, contacts, cases, opportunities — relationship layer), Zendesk (tickets, interactions — service layer), Application Logs (usage events), and Billing System (payment status). We don't query these live — we build a Customer Context API as an aggregation layer with caching. This is API-first so Sales, RevOps, and Product can consume the same data."

Questions to Plant

Look at Caroline: "The hardest integration is the Zendesk-to-Salesforce join. Is there a shared key today, or would we need to build that mapping?" This is the #1 open question — asking it demonstrates integration realism and gives Caroline a moment to contribute expertise. Then to Evan: "The payroll engine API is the long pole — I'd spike on that in Week 1."

Why This Matters

Caroline's rubric evaluates data source awareness, integration realism, schema understanding, and build vs. buy awareness. Naming Salesforce objects (Account, Contact, Case), acknowledging rate limits, proposing the aggregation layer instead of direct queries — these are the signals that score 4-5 on her rubric.

06
Roadmap, Resources & Rollout
Timeline, team, and how we go from "built" to "adopted."
2 min ▼
What to Say

"7 weeks total. Week 0: discovery — shadow agents, validate assumptions, baseline metrics. Week 1: data access spikes to de-risk integrations. Weeks 2-4: build sprint. Week 5: alpha with 5 agents. Week 6: beta with a full pod. Week 7: full rollout with training. Team: 1 backend, 1 frontend, 1 data engineer from Data Services, 1 designer, and me. QA shared. The de-risking spikes in Week 1 determine final scope — if the Salesforce join key doesn't exist, FS-2 degrades to Zendesk-only. We adjust based on reality, not assumptions."

Keep Moving

Don't linger on the resource plan — the detail is in the document. Hit the three things they care about: (1) the timeline is realistic, (2) the hardest problems are front-loaded, (3) there are explicit stage gates before scaling. Then transition to metrics.

07
Success Metrics & What's Next
How we know it worked + the Phase 2-3 vision, planted lightly.
2 min ▼
What to Say

"Primary metrics: AHT reduction 15-20% (less context-switching), FCR improvement 10% (better information), Tool adoption >80% daily active in 30 days (must replace, not supplement). Guardrails: NPS maintains +60, data accuracy ≥95%. The leading indicator I'm watching closest is search-to-context time under 5 seconds — that's the moment the agent knows they have what they need." Then one line on future: "Phase 2 adds health scoring and in-place actions. Phase 3 adds proactive intelligence. The API-first architecture means each phase builds on the last without rearchitecting."

Transition to Workshop

"Everything I've shown you is a hypothesis. I built it from first principles, not from sitting with your agents. Now I want to pressure-test it with the people in this room. Let's shift into workshop mode — I have 45 minutes and 4 exercises designed to validate, challenge, and refine this scope together."

Total presentation time: ~20 minutes. That leaves 10 minutes for Q&A before the workshop. Don't rush to fill 20 minutes — finishing in 17-18 and having a richer Q&A is better than padding.

The Q&A is not a quiz — it's a performance. They're not testing whether you know the answer. They're testing how you think under pressure, whether you can defend decisions without being defensive, and whether you create space for their expertise.

Anticipated Killer Questions 10 min Q&A
Renee Orser — VP, Hiring Manager
"If you had to cut half of your MVP scope, what goes and what stays?"
Answer framework: "The 3 foundation stories (FS-1, FS-2, FS-3) are non-negotiable — they ARE the product. Without instant caller recognition, interaction history, and account status, we haven't built anything worth opening. If I cut half of the problem stories, I keep PS-1 (Pay Stub Context) because payroll discrepancies are the highest-volume call driver and the delta highlighting is the 'wow' moment. Everything else becomes deep links — which still deliver 80% of the value. The architectural investment is identical either way; we're only cutting presentation-layer features, not the data foundation."
Renee Orser
"How does this connect to the broader ITO mandate? This case study is about CSO, but the role also covers Sales tooling."
Answer: "That's exactly why the architecture is API-first. The Customer Context API we're building for CSO is domain-agnostic at the data layer. The same company profiles, account health signals, and interaction history that power the CSO dashboard can feed Sales tooling, RevOps reporting, and Product analytics. We're not building a CSO dashboard — we're building a customer intelligence service. The CSO use case is the first consumer because the pain is most acute and the feedback loop is tightest."
Caroline Crossland — Salesforce Architect
"You've listed 6 data sources for MVP. Which integration is the riskiest and how would you de-risk it?"
Answer: "The Zendesk-to-Salesforce join is the riskiest by far. Both systems store customer data, but if there's no clean shared key — like a mapping between Zendesk Organization ID and Salesforce Account ID — the unified interaction timeline breaks at the foundation. I'd de-risk it in Week 1: a spike where the data engineer queries both systems and attempts the join on a sample of 100 accounts. If the mapping is clean, we proceed. If it's messy, FS-2 degrades to Zendesk-only history in MVP and we add a data cleanup sprint. I'd rather know on Day 5 than Day 25." Then ask: "Is that mapping something your team has already built?"
Annelise Bryda — Staff Designer
"Walk me through what happens in the first 10 seconds after an agent opens this dashboard for a new call."
Answer: "Second 0-2: Agent hits ⌘K or clicks the search bar. Types a name, phone number, or company. Results appear instantly with role badges — admin vs. employee. Second 2-4: Agent clicks the match. The dashboard loads in three tiers — identity card first (name, role, company), then the status strip (plan type, billing, next payroll), then interaction history begins streaming in. Second 4-8: Agent scans left-to-right, top-to-bottom — the same cognitive sequence their brain already follows: WHO is this, WHAT'S the context, WHY might they be calling. By second 10, they can greet the customer by name, acknowledge their last interaction, and orient to their current situation. The information hierarchy mirrors the urgency hierarchy — the most time-critical data loads first."
Evan Lehrman — Sr. Technical PM
"What's the first ticket you'd write for the engineering team on Day 1?"
Answer: "It's a spike, not a feature ticket. Title: 'Spike: Validate Zendesk-Salesforce Account Mapping.' Acceptance criteria: (1) Query 100 Zendesk organizations and attempt to match each to a Salesforce Account record. (2) Document the match rate, the key used, and any data quality issues. (3) Produce a go/no-go recommendation for the unified interaction timeline. Time-boxed: 2 days. Why this first? Because it's the single decision with the highest scope impact. If the answer is 'clean mapping exists,' we build as planned. If not, we adjust MVP scope before committing engineering effort to the wrong path."
Evan Lehrman
"How do you prioritize — what framework do you use?"
Answer: "For this proposal I used ICE — Impact, Confidence, Ease. I chose it over RICE because Reach is misleading for internal tools — all agents are the audience, so reach is constant. But I adapted the framework to context: for an internal tool MVP, I weight 'agent workflow impact' higher than 'business intelligence value' because adoption is the biggest risk. The best health dashboard in the world fails if agents don't open it. So features that make the agent's core job faster and easier beat features that provide strategic insights — those come in Phase 2 after adoption is earned."

Meta-strategy for all Q&A: When you don't know the answer, say so directly and turn it into a question: "That's exactly the kind of thing I'd want to discover in Week 0. What does that look like here?" Honesty + curiosity scores higher than confident guessing.

Mode shift: You are no longer the presenter. You are the facilitator. Your job is to create the conditions for the panel to contribute their expertise, arrive at a shared MVP definition, and feel ownership over the outcome. If you talk more than 30% of the workshop, you're doing it wrong.

Pre-Work: Whiteboard Setup

Before the interview starts, set up the whiteboard with 3 zones. This signals preparation and gives the group a visual anchor from minute one.

Whiteboard Layout — Pre-Drawn
✅ IN (MVP)
← items move here during Exercise 3
◐ LATER (Phase 2-3)
← deferred items with rationale
✗ OUT (Not Building)
← explicit cuts with reason
Workshop Exercises — Minute by Minute 45 Minutes Total
0:00–3:00
Opening — Set the Frame
Shift the energy from "watching a presentation" to "building together"
▼
Script

"We've covered the strategy. Now I want to get practical — what's the smallest thing we can ship that delivers real value to CSO agents? I have a proposed MVP scope, but it's built on assumptions. Several of you have context I don't. So here's how I'd like to use the next 45 minutes: four exercises, each one building on the last, ending with a shared view of what's in, what's out, and what comes later. Ground rules: all ideas are valid, I'll capture everything on the whiteboard, and we'll converge at the end. Sound good?"

Goal

Permission to lead. Buy-in on the structure. Energy shift from passive to active. Engage Renee early — make eye contact when you say "sound good?" She's the decision-maker.

If Energy Is Low

Don't ask "any questions before we start?" — that kills momentum. Instead go straight into Exercise 1 with a direct question to a specific person.

3:00–13:00
Exercise 1 — Validate the Agent Journey
Map the current workflow, surface what you got right and wrong
▼
Facilitation Prompts

Draw a simple timeline on the whiteboard: "Call comes in → Agent resolves → Call ends." Then ask targeted questions to specific people:

Ask Renee (first)

"Renee, you're closest to the CSO operation. When an agent picks up the phone today, what systems do they actually open? What's the first thing they do?" — This validates or invalidates the 5-6 system assumption. Let her talk. Write what she says on the whiteboard.

Ask Evan

"Evan, from the product side — where do agents get stuck? What's the most common point where they have to put a customer on hold or escalate?" — This validates the problem ranking. If he names something you didn't anticipate, write it down and say "that's a good one — let's factor that in."

Ask Caroline

"Caroline, the Salesforce data — does the CSO team interact with Salesforce directly today, or is it mainly used by Sales and RevOps?" — This reveals whether Salesforce is a current pain point for agents or just a data source.

Ask Annelise

"Annelise, have you done any research or observation with CSO agents? What's the biggest UX pain point you've seen?" — This gives the designer the floor and signals that you value her perspective. If she hasn't, that's fine — redirect to what she'd want to know.

What to Capture

On the whiteboard, annotate the timeline with the actual systems, pain points, and bottlenecks the panel identifies. Circle the 2-3 biggest pain points. These become the validation for your MVP scope — or the signal that you need to adjust it.

If You Were Wrong About Something

This is the best possible outcome. Say: "That's different from what I assumed — and that's exactly why we do this exercise. Let me adjust." Write the correction on the whiteboard. The rubric says exceptional means "creates genuine collaborative energy" — learning in real-time from the panel IS that energy.

13:00–25:00
Exercise 2 — Data & Feature Prioritization
Use the prototype as a reaction surface. Vote on what matters most.
▼
Setup

"Based on what we just mapped, let me show you where I landed on MVP features — and I want your honest reaction." Pull up the prototype on screen. Walk through it briefly (2 min max), then shift to structured feedback.

Structured Voting

Write 9 features on the whiteboard (FS-1, FS-2, FS-3, PS-1 through PS-6). Give each person 5 dots (use different-colored markers). "Put your dots on the features you believe deliver the most value to an agent's Day 1 experience." This surfaces disagreement productively — if Caroline puts all her dots on data features and Annelise puts hers on UX features, you have a conversation to facilitate.

After Voting — Probe the Divergences

Look for split votes. "Caroline, you put 3 dots on FS-2 — interaction history. Tell us why that's most important." Then: "Annelise, you weighted PS-1 — the pay stub viewer. What makes that the Day 1 win?" Each person explains their reasoning. You're not resolving the disagreement — you're surfacing the tradeoff and letting the group inform the decision.

Caroline's Moment

When data architecture comes up: "Caroline, which of these data sources can we access without building new integrations? Where do we need your team's help?" This gives her a concrete contribution and reveals real constraints.

Evan's Moment

"Evan, gut check — could we ship the top-voted features in 5 weeks with 2 engineers and a data engineer?" This gives him the technical feasibility role. If he says "that's tight," ask "what would you cut to make it realistic?"

If Panel Adds Features You Didn't Propose

Write them on the whiteboard in a "NEW" column. Say: "That's a great addition. Let's put it on the board and we'll decide in/out/later in Exercise 3." Never dismiss an idea — capture it and let the group decide its priority.

25:00–37:00
Exercise 3 — Scope Lock & Wireframe Refinement
Move items to IN/LATER/OUT. Hand Annelise the marker.
▼
Setup

"Alright, we've validated the journey, we've voted on features. Now let's lock scope. I'm going to read each feature and we'll move it to IN, LATER, or OUT based on what we've discussed." Go through each feature. For anything with strong consensus, move it fast. For anything contentious, pause for 60 seconds of discussion and then make a call: "I'm hearing X and Y. For MVP, I'd propose [decision] because [rationale]. Does that work?"

Annelise's Moment — The Marker

Once scope is roughly locked, hand Annelise a marker (or share the Miro board, if remote). Say: "Annelise, given what we've prioritized, how would you think about the information hierarchy? What should an agent see first, second, third?" Let her sketch. Ask questions, don't direct. If she draws something different from your prototype, say "I like that — the progressive disclosure there is better than what I had." Designers engage best when they're drawing, not listening.

Facilitation Questions During Sketch

While Annelise is drawing, ask the group: "What happens if the caller isn't in the system?" (edge case). "What if the company has 500 employees — does the UI scale?" (Evan's territory). "Should the layout change for inbound call vs. proactive outreach?" (Renee's territory). These questions keep everyone engaged while Annelise is at the board.

If Time Is Running Short

Skip the wireframe refinement and focus on scope lock. A clear IN/LATER/OUT board with group consensus is more valuable than a partially-discussed sketch. Say: "Let's make sure we're aligned on scope — I can take the wireframe offline with Annelise."

37:00–45:00
Exercise 4 — Top Risks & Alignment
Name the risks, confirm the scope, close strong.
▼
Identify Top 3 Risks

"Before we close — what are the top 3 things that could derail this?" Go around the room. Write each risk on the whiteboard. You should have your own answers ready (Zendesk join key, payroll API access, agent adoption) but let the panel name theirs first. If they say something you didn't consider, that's gold — capture it visibly.

Alignment Check

Point to the IN/LATER/OUT board. Read back the MVP scope: "So our Phase 1 MVP is: [list the IN items]. We've deferred [list the LATER items] for Phase 2. And we've explicitly cut [OUT items]. Is everyone comfortable that this is the right starting point?" Pause for nods or objections. If someone pushes back, engage — don't steamroll. If there's genuine disagreement, say: "That's a valid tension. Here's how I'd resolve it in Week 1: [specific de-risking action]."

Closing Statement (Final 60 Seconds)

"Thank you for this — genuinely. This is the kind of cross-functional scoping session that makes products better. What we've built in 45 minutes is a validated MVP scope, informed by the people who understand the data architecture, the design constraints, the technical feasibility, and the strategic context. My first action in the role would be the Week 0 discovery — shadow agents, validate today's work with real observation, and come back with an updated scope ready to sprint on. I'm excited about what this team can build together."

What "Exceptional" Looks Like (From the Rubric)

Renee sees: "I can picture this person running a roadmap review with my VP peers." Caroline, Annelise, and Evan each feel: "This person gets MY world." The group feels: "We own the MVP output — we weren't led to a predetermined answer." That's the target state. If the final scope differs from your proposal because the panel contributed new information, that's not a failure — it's the point.

Contingency Plans
IF
One person dominates the conversation
▼
Response

Acknowledge their point, then redirect: "That's a really strong point, [name]. I want to make sure we capture [other person]'s perspective too — [other person], how does that land from the [design/data/product] angle?" Use the structure to distribute airtime. The voting exercise naturally creates balanced contribution.

IF
The group disagrees sharply on scope
▼
Response

Name the disagreement explicitly: "I'm hearing two perspectives — [X view] and [Y view]. Both are valid. Here's what I'd propose: let's put this in 'IN with a spike' — we build toward it but the Week 1 data access spike determines whether it ships in MVP or slides to Phase 2." Turn disagreements into de-risking actions, not debates to win.

IF
An exercise runs long and you're behind on time
▼
Response

Priority order if time is tight: (1) Exercise 1 (validate the journey) is non-negotiable — it earns credibility. (2) Exercise 3 (scope lock) is the deliverable — the group needs to leave with alignment. (3) Exercise 4 closing can be compressed to 2 minutes. What gets cut first: the wireframe refinement in Exercise 3 (Annelise can do it offline) and the formal dot-voting in Exercise 2 (switch to verbal prioritization). Never cut the closing — always read back the scope and ask for alignment.

IF
Someone reveals that an assumption is completely wrong
▼
Response

Treat it as a gift, not a threat: "That changes things — and that's exactly why this workshop matters. Let me update the board." Cross out the wrong assumption. Write the correct information. Ask: "Given this, should we adjust the MVP scope? What moves?" The rubric scores how you handle surprise information — composure + adaptation is the top mark. The architecture is designed to absorb scope changes. That's the whole point of the phased, de-risked approach.

IF
They ask you to solve something on the spot you haven't prepared for
▼
Response

Don't fake it. Say: "I don't have a prepared answer for that, but here's how I'd think about it..." then reason through it live. Use the whiteboard. Ask the panel for input. Transparent thinking under pressure scores higher than a polished but inauthentic answer. If it's a deep technical question outside your expertise, say: "That's a great question for the discovery sprint — I'd want to dig into that with [relevant expert] in Week 1."
