Customer Central — Prioritization

Why This Order

The six jobs aren't a menu to pick from — they're a dependency chain. Some jobs are impossible without others existing first. The phasing isn't a preference. It's physics.

The Dependency Principle

You cannot diagnose a problem if you don't know who the customer is or what's happened before. You cannot assess account health if you haven't consolidated the data that health is computed from. You cannot pre-empt a problem if you can't yet see the signals that predict it. You cannot recommend a product expansion if you don't understand what they already use and how they use it.

Jobs 1 and 2 are the data foundation. Everything above them is impossible — not just harder, impossible — without that foundation in place and trusted.

The Dependency Logic, Step by Step
F
FOUNDATION · NO DEPENDENCIES

Jobs 1 & 2 — Data Consolidation

These two jobs are pure data unification. They don't require any prior capability to exist — they require integrations to data sources (Salesforce, Zendesk, production DB) and a presentation layer.

Job 1 consolidates the customer's identity and profile: who they are, what company, what product, what state the account is in. Job 2 consolidates their interaction history: every call, email, and chat across every channel and agent.

Together, these create the unified customer record that every other job reads from. Without this record, every other job is either impossible or requires the agent to build the picture manually — which is exactly the problem we're solving.

O
OPERATIONS · REQUIRES FOUNDATION

Job 3 — Handle the Direct Problem

An agent cannot diagnose a payroll discrepancy without first knowing which company, which employee, and what plan they're on (Job 1). They cannot avoid asking the customer to repeat themselves without seeing prior interactions (Job 2).

✕ Can't diagnose without Job 1 ✕ Can't contextualize without Job 2

Resolution also means knowing what's already been tried. If the customer called yesterday and an agent applied a fix that hasn't taken effect yet — the current agent needs to know that before attempting the same fix again. That's Job 2 enabling Job 3.

Job 4 — Understand Status & Health

Account status (billing current? tickets open? payroll on schedule?) is computed from the same data that Jobs 1 and 2 consolidate. You're not looking at new data — you're interpreting the same data through an evaluative lens.

✕ Can't compute status without Job 1 profile data ✕ Can't assess health without Job 2 interaction patterns

Account health now includes a lightweight composite score in the MVP — a rule-based weighted formula: Base = (Billing x 0.30) + (Payroll x 0.25) + (Support x 0.20) + (Satisfaction x 0.15) + (Activity x 0.10), then Final = Base × Tenure Factor. Tenure Factor: <30 days = 0.75, 30-90 days = 0.85, 90-180 days = 0.95, 180+ days = 1.00. This produces transparent, decomposable health bands agents can verify against their own intuition. The data doesn't need 60 days of validation to be scored — it needs to exist and be queryable, which Jobs 1 and 2 ensure. Advanced ML-weighted adaptive scoring — where agents can't verify the logic themselves — requires validated data patterns and remains Phase 2.

I
INTELLIGENCE · REQUIRES FOUNDATION + OPERATIONS

Job 5 — Pre-empt Problems

Pre-emption requires pattern recognition across time. "This company has had manual payroll adjustments 3 cycles in a row" requires interaction history (Job 2). "Declining login frequency" requires engagement tracking that builds on the profile (Job 1). "At-risk account" requires a health model (Job 4) to define what "at-risk" means.

✕ Can't detect patterns without Job 2 history ✕ Can't define "at-risk" without Job 4 health model ✕ Can't target alerts without Job 1 profile data

Job 6 — Expand the Relationship

You cannot recommend "upgrade to PEO Plus" without knowing they're on PEO Basic (Job 1). You cannot suggest "companies your size typically use time tracking" without knowing their employee count and current feature usage (Job 1). You cannot time the recommendation without knowing they're a healthy, growing account (Job 4) — recommending an upsell to a churning customer is tone-deaf.

✕ Can't recommend without Job 1 product context ✕ Can't time it without Job 4 health signals ✕ Can't frame it without Job 2 relationship history

There's Also an Adoption Argument

Beyond technical dependencies, there's a behavioral reason to sequence this way. Internal tools live or die on adoption. You earn trust in layers:

PHASE 1
Earn trust through accuracy
The data is correct. The profile matches reality. The timeline is complete. A lightweight health score gives agents a verifiable at-a-glance assessment. Agents believe the tool because it's right every time — and they can check the health score against their own intuition.
PHASE 1 → PHASE 2
Earn trust through transparent insight, then upgrade
Phase 1 earns trust with a simple, transparent, rule-based health score agents can verify against their own intuition. Phase 2 upgrades to ML-weighted adaptive scoring that agents trust because the simple version proved accurate. Trust in transparent rules precedes trust in opaque models.
PHASE 3
Earn trust through prediction
Only after agents trust both the data and the analysis will they act on predictions — proactive alerts, expansion signals. Premature predictions destroy credibility.
The Phases
MVP

Unified Context

Job 1 · Job 2 · Job 3 (partial) · Job 4 (status + lightweight health score)
Replaces the multi-system workflow. Agents open one tool instead of six. The data is consolidated, accurate, and fast. Adoption is earned.
Phase 2

Health & Action

Job 3 (full resolution) · Job 4 (advanced ML-weighted scoring) · Job 5 (early signals)
Agents now act from within the tool. ML-weighted adaptive scoring upgrades the simple health score agents already trust. The tool becomes the workspace, not just the reference.
Phase 3

Proactive Intelligence

Job 5 (full pre-emption) · Job 6 (expansion)
The system tells agents what's coming and what to recommend. CSO shifts from reactive support to proactive partnership. The moat widens.

The phasing isn't a preference. It's a dependency chain.

Every phase builds what the next phase needs to exist. You cannot skip a layer without building on an unstable foundation. The MVP isn't the most important features first — it's the features that make every future feature possible.

This is also why the architecture matters in Phase 1. We build the data layer as a normalized, API-first service — not because the MVP needs it, but because Phases 2 and 3 are inevitable once the foundation proves its value. Building the right foundation means the future arrives as iteration, not re-architecture.
