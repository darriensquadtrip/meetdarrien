This is your speaker's guide. Every beat is timed, every transition is scripted, every panelist engagement point is mapped. Each section answers the question the previous one raises. "Feel the pain" creates urgency. "Name the problems" creates structure. "Show the logic" creates trust in the process. "Define the MVP" makes it concrete. "Prove it's buildable" removes doubt. "Show the plan" creates confidence. "Invite collaboration" transitions to the workshop.

Presentation principle: They've read the proposal. Don't re-read it to them. The presentation is about why — the reasoning behind decisions, the assumptions you're testing, and the tradeoffs you made. Move fast on facts. Go deep on thinking.

The Encounter Narrative — Today's Reality

▼
Script

"Before the solution, the problem. Let me describe what I believe a CSO agent's experience looks like today — and I want you to correct me where I'm wrong.

T+0 seconds: Phone rings. Agent sees a phone number — maybe just a number, maybe a name from caller ID. They answer.
T+10s: 'Hi, this is Sarah from Acme Corp.' Agent types 'Acme Corp' into Salesforce. Waits.

The before/after structure is the single most effective persuasion technique for internal tools. Decision-makers don't buy features — they buy the delta between pain and relief. By narrating both in seconds, you've made the value tangible without a single metric.

What to Avoid

Don't belabor the ideal encounter. Keep it shorter than the "before" — the brevity IS the point. The whole thing resolves in 30 seconds. That asymmetry — 120s of pain vs. 30s of resolution — makes the argument visually.

Section 2 Name the Problems — Structured Analysis 3:00 – 6:00

Purpose: Impose structure on the messy problem space. Show you can decompose ambiguity into actionable categories. Name the business opportunity — revenue, retention, and scale.

Speak
The 6 Compounding Problems
90 seconds. Name each quickly. Don't elaborate — the document has the detail.
▼
Script

"I've identified six problems that compound on each other. Quick overview — the detail is in the proposal:

One. The context-switching tax. Agents toggle between 5-6 systems during a single call. Each switch costs 10-15 seconds of cognitive load. Across thousands of daily interactions, this compounds into massive productivity loss.

Two. The caller identity problem. When a call comes in, the agent doesn't know: is this an admin or an employee? What company? What history? The first 30-60 seconds are wasted on identification — and these two caller types need completely different information surfaced.

Three. The 'repeat yourself' problem. A customer who emailed Monday, chatted Wednesday, and calls Friday has explained their issue three times. This is the number-one destroyer of the Justworks brand promise.

Four. The knowledge gap. Payroll, benefits, HR, compliance across 50 states and multiple carriers. No agent can hold it all. When they don't know, they escalate — reducing first-contact resolution.

Five. The proactive blindspot. The prompt explicitly asks for pre-emptive support. This doesn't exist today. The data for proactive insights is spread across systems, never synthesized into agent-facing signals.

Six. The scaling threat. 10,000+ customers and growing. Tribal knowledge doesn't scale. Without tooling investment, quality degrades or headcount grows linearly. Neither is sustainable."

Delivery Note

This is rapid-fire. Number each problem clearly. Use your fingers if standing. The energy here is analytical — you're imposing structure. Don't pause between problems; the momentum conveys that you've done the work.

What to Avoid

Don't elaborate on each one — the document does that. Don't say "I assume" or "I think" — state them as hypotheses you're testing. "I've identified" is stronger than "I believe."

Speak
The Business Opportunity — Why This Matters Strategically
45 seconds. Connect problems to business outcomes.
▼
Script

"These problems aren't just operational pain — they're strategic risk. Justworks' customer service is its primary market differentiator. Nine consecutive Stevie Awards, NPS of +60, referenced in every competitive analysis. That differentiator is currently held together by great people, not great tools. Great people with great tools is strictly better than great people with fragmented tools. And as Justworks scales from 10,000 to 50,000 customers, the moat either widens with tooling investment or erodes without it. Customer Central is how the moat widens."

Renee Hears

The strategic framing she needs for her VP conversations. This isn't a feature request — it's infrastructure for the competitive moat. She can picture using this exact language in a roadmap review with her peers.

What to Avoid

Don't recite company facts back at them — they know their revenue and customer count. Don't mention salary context or your background here. The business opportunity speaks for itself when connected to their own stated differentiator.

Section 3 Show the Logic — How I Got to the MVP 6:00 – 10:00

Purpose: This is the section they can't get from the document. Show the reasoning chain — the methodology that converts ambiguity into a defensible scope. This is where Renee sees strategic thinking under ambiguity and Evan sees product craft.

Speak
The Methodology — Problems First, Features Never
60 seconds. The philosophical frame that governed every decision.
▼
Script

"I didn't start with features. I started with the prompt's own language. The case study describes 6 distinct jobs Customer Central needs to enable — from carrying out routine tasks to understanding customer profiles to pre-empting problems. I extracted those jobs, then asked: what specific problems prevent agents from doing each job today?

That gave me 20 specific CSO problems and 12 health questions — not a feature wish list, but a problem inventory. I scored each on Impact, Confidence, and Ease — and discovered something I didn't expect."

Reference Artifacts

The JTBD framework (justworks-jtbd.jsx) traces each job to exact prompt language. The ICE scoring matrix (justworks-ice-scoring.jsx) shows the full problem inventory with scores. Don't open these artifacts on screen unless someone asks — just reference them verbally. The thinking matters more than the spreadsheet.

Why This Matters

Most candidates will propose features they think are cool. You're showing a rigorous methodology: prompt → jobs → problems → scoring → scope. That's the GPM thinking Renee is hiring for. Evan sees a PM who works from problems, not assumptions.

Show
The Convergence Discovery
60 seconds. The "aha" moment that validated the foundation.
▼
Script

"When I scored everything, something interesting happened. The 20 CSO problems and the 12 health questions were scored independently — different lists, different lenses. But when I looked at what data categories both lists needed, they converged on the same 6 data foundations: company identity and plan, contact identity and role, interaction history, account billing status, recent activity events, and product configuration.

Two independent analyses pointing to the same infrastructure. That convergence told me the foundation is sound — we're not building based on one perspective. The same data layer that enables reactive support also enables proactive health monitoring. That's not an accident; it's validation."

What Each Person Hears

Renee: Rigorous process, not gut instinct. Caroline: Clear data categories she can map to sources. Evan: A PM who validates before committing. Annelise: User needs driving architecture, not the reverse.

Speak
The Trust Progression — Why Three Phases
45 seconds. The strategic framework for phasing.
▼
Script

"The three-phase roadmap isn't arbitrary. It follows a trust progression.

Phase 1: Earn trust through accuracy — and a transparent score. Show agents the data they already know is correct — their own company, their own interactions. If the data is right, they trust the tool. If it's wrong even once, they never come back. And because we're already surfacing the 5 key signals — billing, payroll, support tickets, satisfaction, activity — we compose them into a lightweight health score. Rule-based, transparent, decomposable. The agent can look at each input and verify the score against their intuition. In Week 2, we sit with experienced agents and ask: 'does this match what you know about this account?' Target: 80% alignment or better. Once they trust the simple score, Phase 2 can add adaptive weighting.

Phase 2: Earn trust through adaptive analysis. Once agents trust the data and the lightweight score, we upgrade to ML-weighted adaptive scoring, predictive churn signals, and automated outreach triggers. Trust in the simple score precedes trust in the adaptive model.

Phase 3: Earn trust through prediction. Only after agents trust the data and the analysis will they act on predictions — proactive alerts, upsell signals. Premature predictions destroy credibility.

Each phase earns the right to the next."

Why This Framing Wins

It shows you understand internal tool adoption — the #1 risk. We earn trust with a transparent, rule-based score agents can verify against their intuition. The lightweight health score ships in Phase 1 because the same 5 signals + tenure adjustment from the status strip compose into a single number the agent reads in 1 second. Formula: Base = (Billing × 0.30) + (Payroll × 0.25) + (Support × 0.20) + (Satisfaction × 0.15) + (Activity × 0.10), then Final = Base × Tenure Factor. Tenure Factor: <30 days = 0.75, 30-90 days = 0.85, 90-180 days = 0.95, 180+ days = 1.00. Phase 2 upgrades the scoring model — not introduces it.

Speak
The Tiebreaker Principle
30 seconds. The architectural philosophy in one sentence.
▼
Script

"One more principle that governed scoping decisions. When two features are equal on effort and near-term value, I pick the one that also builds toward the AI-native future. Structured interaction records over free-text notes. Normalized schema over flat aggregation. API-first service over monolithic dashboard. Same effort, wildly different future leverage. You're not building an AI system in Phase 1 — you're building the data foundation that makes Phase 3 inevitable rather than a rearchitecture."

Delivery Note

This is the most quotable line in the presentation. Slow down on "same effort, wildly different future leverage." It's the kind of statement that Renee will remember. But land it once and move on — don't oversell the AI angle. The prompt says small businesses should work with real people.

Section 4 Define the MVP — What We Build 10:00 – 13:00

Purpose: Make the abstract concrete. Name the 11 features. Explain the Full Build vs. Deep Link framework. Demo the prototype for 60 seconds. This is where Evan evaluates requirements specificity and Annelise evaluates information design.

Speak
The 11 Features — Full Build vs. Deep Link + Health Score
90 seconds. Name them fast. Explain the framework.
▼
Script

"Eleven features in four categories. Three foundation stories that every interaction needs, six problem-specific stories that address the highest-volume call drivers, and two health scoring features that turn raw signals into actionable intelligence.

Foundation stories — full builds: FS-1, Instant Caller Recognition — search by name, phone, email, results in under 2 seconds. FS-2, Complete Interaction History — merged timeline from Zendesk and Salesforce, all channels, all agents. FS-3, Account Status Strip — billing, payroll date, open tickets, recent changes at a glance.

Problem stories — a mix of full builds and deep links: PS-1, Pay Stub Context — full build, because payroll discrepancies are the highest-volume call driver and the period-over-period delta highlighting is a novel capability. PS-2 through PS-5 — deep links. One click to the right screen in the right source system. Deep links deliver 80% of the value at 20% of the engineering cost.

Health scoring: We're also shipping a lightweight health score. HS-1, Lightweight Health Score — same 5 signals + tenure adjustment from the status strip, composed into a single number the agent reads in 1 second. Rule-based, transparent, agent-verifiable. Formula: Base = billing times 0.30 + payroll times 0.25 + support times 0.20 + satisfaction times 0.15 + activity times 0.10, then Final = Base times Tenure Factor. Five color bands from green to red. HS-2, Leadership Health Overview — the same score the agent sees, but aggregated across the portfolio so the CSO head can spot at-risk accounts without asking anyone.

The acceptance criteria are in the document — specific thresholds Evan could write tickets from today. Search under 2 seconds. Dashboard load under 3 seconds. Data refresh every 5 minutes."

Why "Full Build vs. Deep Link"

This is the MVP discipline the rubric evaluates. You're showing you can make hard cuts without cutting value. Deep links are still better than what agents have today — one click vs. manually navigating. This framework also gives Phase 2 a natural upgrade path: convert deep links to full builds based on usage data.

What to Avoid

Don't read every acceptance criterion aloud. Don't explain each deep link in detail — the document does that. The phrase "Evan could write tickets from today" is a deliberate signal to Evan that you know what PM-Engineering collaboration looks like.

Section 5 Prove It's Buildable — Data & Architecture 13:00 – 16:00

Purpose: This is for Caroline and Evan. Show technical depth without over-engineering. Name the data sources, the integration approach, and the hardest problems. Ask questions that demonstrate you know what you don't know.

Speak
Data Architecture — 5 Sources, 1 API Layer
90 seconds. The technical backbone.
▼
Script

"Five source systems feed the MVP. Production DB — companies, members, payroll runs, billing. This is the source of truth for product data. Salesforce — Accounts, Contacts, Cases, Opportunities. The relationship layer, Caroline's domain. Zendesk — tickets, users, satisfaction ratings. The service layer. Application logs — login events, feature usage. And the billing system — payment status, invoice history.

We don't query these systems live during a call — that's fragile and slow. Instead, we build a Customer Context API as an aggregation layer. Normalized schema, cached data with freshness timestamps, API-first so the same service can feed Sales tooling, RevOps reporting, and Product analytics. The CSO dashboard is the first consumer, not the only one."

Why "API-First" Matters

This single architectural decision signals platform thinking to Renee. It also answers the question she'll have about the broader ITO mandate — the same data layer serves multiple teams. Caroline hears that you're building WITH Salesforce (as a source), not around it.

Engage
Plant the Open Questions — Caroline and Evan's Moments
60 seconds. Ask the questions that show integration realism.
▼
Script

"There are integration challenges I want to surface now — some of which you can likely answer.

Caroline — the hardest integration is the Zendesk-to-Salesforce join. The unified interaction timeline depends on a clean mapping between Zendesk Organization IDs and Salesforce Account records. Is that mapping something your team has already built, or would we need to create it?

Evan — the payroll engine data is the long pole for PS-1, the pay stub viewer. I'd spike on that in Week 1 — if there's no clean API and we're looking at raw database queries, PS-1 might downgrade to a deep link for MVP. Do you know the current access pattern for payroll data from internal tools?

I've documented 14 open questions in the proposal, organized by who can answer them. Several of these we can resolve right here in the workshop."

Why Ask Questions During the Presentation

Three effects at once. (1) It demonstrates integration realism — you're not hand-waving about "pulling data from systems." (2) It gives Caroline and Evan floor time early, which the rubric rewards. (3) If they answer, you get real information that sharpens the workshop. If they defer, you've still shown the right questions. Either outcome scores well.

If They Answer

Listen. Don't rush past their answer to get back to your script. If Caroline says "yes, that mapping exists" — say "that's great news, it de-risks FS-2 significantly." If she says "it's messy" — say "good to know now rather than in Week 3. That's exactly why we spike in Week 1." Adapt in real-time. The presentation is a conversation, not a monologue.

Section 6 Show the Plan — Roadmap, Resources, Rollout 16:00 – 18:00

Purpose: Prove this is executable, not theoretical. Hit the three things they care about: timeline is realistic, hardest problems are front-loaded, stage gates prevent bad rollouts. Then metrics.

Speak
Timeline & Team — 7 Weeks, 3 Engineers + Designer + PM + Shared QA
45 seconds. Concise. Detail is in the document.
▼
Script

"Seven calendar weeks, total. Week 0 is pure discovery — I'd spend the first 3 days shadowing CSO agents to validate everything I've proposed today. Week 1: four data access spikes to de-risk the hardest integrations before we commit scope. Weeks 2-4: build sprint. Week 5: alpha with 5 volunteer agents and daily feedback. Week 6: beta with a full team of 20-30 agents. Week 7: full rollout with training.

Team: 2 full-stack software engineers — one owns Search & Identity end-to-end, one owns Panels & History end-to-end. 1 data engineer from Data Services — that's a critical resource I'd want to confirm availability for. 1 designer, ideally from Annelise's team, and me. QA shared.

Three things make this realistic: the de-risking spikes in Week 1 determine final scope — we adjust based on reality, not assumptions. The stage gates between alpha, beta, and rollout mean we don't scale until quality is proven. And in Week 2, we run a health score validation exercise — we sit with experienced agents, show them the lightweight scores for accounts they know well, and ask: 'does this match your intuition?' Target is 80% or better alignment. If the score doesn't pass that bar, we recalibrate the weights before beta. That's how we de-risk the scoring model without waiting for months of production data."

Keep Moving

Don't linger. The detail is in the phased-roadmap artifact. Hit the three signals: (1) discovery before building, (2) front-loaded risk, (3) quality gates. Then transition to metrics.

Speak
Success Metrics — How We Know It Worked
45 seconds. Primary, guardrail, and the leading indicator.
▼
Script

"Three layers of metrics. Primary: Average Handle Time — target 15-20% reduction within 90 days. First Contact Resolution — target 10% improvement. Tool adoption — over 80% daily active usage within 30 days. The tool must replace the existing workflow, not supplement it.

Guardrails: NPS maintains at +60 or above. Data accuracy at 95% or better — agents seeing wrong data is worse than seeing no data.

The leading indicator I'm watching closest: search-to-context time under 5 seconds. That's the moment the agent has what they need. If that metric is healthy, everything downstream follows."

Why "Search-to-Context Time"

This metric is unusual — most candidates will list AHT and FCR and stop. Search-to-context time is a product metric, not just a business metric. It tells you whether the tool is working at the moment of truth. It's also the fastest feedback loop — measurable from Day 1 of alpha, unlike AHT which needs weeks of data for significance.

Prep
If You Finish Early — What to Do With Extra Time
▼
Options (In Priority Order)

Best case: finish at 17-18 min. Open the floor: "I'd love to hear initial reactions before we shift into the workshop." This creates a natural Q&A that's more conversational than formal. If Renee has a question, she'll ask it now. If not, the extra time flows into the workshop, which can always use more time.

Do NOT: pad by showing more artifacts, re-explaining features, or adding tangents. Conciseness is a signal of confidence. Filling time is a signal of anxiety.

▼
Cut Order (First to Cut → Last to Cut)

First cut: §6 metrics detail — say "AHT, FCR, adoption — targets and rationale are in the document" (saves 45s).
